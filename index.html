Studio Ghibli: A Non-Commercial Centimillionaire











Introduction



The global animation industry is an example for market structure, where firms range from standalone producers to global conglomerates. It has a unique position as a high-quality, artist-driven studio in a competitive global market.
Studio Ghibli's net worth is around $245 million.
In this market, Studio Ghibli acts as a monopolistic competition, excelling with its strong brand identity and limited output. In comparison, oligopolistic giants such as Disney, Pixar and Netflix dominate the economy through vertical integration (i.e., multiple stages of production or distribution within its own supply chain).
The animation market is inaccesible to new firms because of high barriers to entry, including high fixed costs, technical expertise, and long production timelines needed. So why does Studio Ghibli stay old-fashioned and give up some of its chances to become a monopolizer?
Studio Ghibli caps their merchandise income at 10 billion yen, in fear that any more commercialization would make their characters 'die instantly'.
Studio Ghibli relies on non-price competition (i.e., a company attracts customers without price changes) through its aesthetics and emotional narratives that cultivate long-term consumer loyalty. The business strategies of Studio Ghibli also applies to concepts such as supply and demand, discretionary funds and behavioral economics.
The animation sector illustrates the tension between creativity and technological advancements, between creative integrity and the maximization of profit in the global economy.
